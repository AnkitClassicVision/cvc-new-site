Dull Pain Behind Your Eyes? Here's Why

Dr. Mital Patel, OD

March 15, 2026

That dull ache behind your eye that builds through the afternoon. The sharp stab that hits without warning. The pressure that makes it hard to focus on your screen, your book, or even the road ahead of you.

If you have been searching for answers about pain behind the eye, you are not alone. It is one of the most common symptoms patients bring to our offices at Classic Vision Care, and we understand the anxiety that comes with it. Online search results range from reassuring to alarming, and the gap between "it's probably eye strain" and "it could be a medical emergency" can feel overwhelming.

Here is what you should know up front: most causes of pain behind the eye are treatable, and many are preventable. As the American Academy of Ophthalmology notes, "where you feel pain is not necessarily an indicator of what's causing the pain." That means the key to relief is understanding the possible causes, knowing which symptoms deserve urgent attention, and taking practical steps to protect your eyes.

This guide walks you through all of it.

What Does Pain Behind the Eye Feel Like?

Is It a Dull Ache, Sharp Pain, or Pressure?

Not all pain behind the eye feels the same, and the character of your pain can help narrow down the cause.

A dull, aching pressure that builds over hours is the most common type. It often starts partway through a long workday or reading session and gradually spreads across the forehead or temples. This pattern typically points to digital eye strain or dry eye.

A sharp, stabbing pain that arrives suddenly and intensely may signal a migraine or, less commonly, a cluster headache. This type of pain often affects one side and can come with nausea, light sensitivity, or tearing in the affected eye.

A deep, constant pressure that worsens when you bend forward or lie down often has sinus involvement. Your sinus cavities sit directly behind and around your eye sockets, and inflammation in those spaces can radiate pressure into the eye area.

The dull ache you feel after six hours at your laptop is very different from the sharp stab that wakes you at 2 a.m. Paying attention to when the pain starts, how long it lasts, and what makes it better or worse gives you and your eye doctor valuable clues about the source.

Does It Matter Which Eye Hurts?

Pain that consistently affects one eye can point toward conditions like migraine, cluster headache, or optic neuritis. Pain in both eyes is more commonly associated with eye strain, dry eye, or sinus congestion. However, the side of the pain alone is not enough to determine the cause. It is one piece of the puzzle, not the whole picture.

It also helps to notice whether the pain stays in the same eye or alternates between sides. A fixed pattern (always the left eye, always in the evening) gives your doctor more to work with than pain that seems random.

What Causes Pain Behind the Eye?

The causes range from everyday strain to conditions that need professional attention. Here are the six most common reasons patients experience this symptom.

Could It Be Digital Eye Strain?

Digital eye strain is the leading cause of the dull, aching pressure that builds behind the eyes during the workday. According to the American Optometric Association, the average American worker spends seven hours a day on a computer. That sustained near-focus work forces a small muscle inside the eye, called the ciliary muscle, to contract continuously. Over time, that muscle fatigues, and the result is the familiar ache behind your eyes, blurry distance vision when you finally look up, and tension in your neck and shoulders.

You may also notice that the pain tends to be worse on days when you skip breaks, work in a room with overhead fluorescent lighting, or use a screen with the brightness turned up too high. All of these factors compound the strain on your visual system.

An outdated glasses or contact lens prescription can make digital eye strain significantly worse. If your eyes are working harder than they should to bring text into focus, the fatigue sets in faster and the pain becomes more persistent. The National Eye Institute identifies uncorrected refractive errors as the most common vision problem in the United States, which means many people are straining without realizing their prescription needs an update.

Is Dry Eye Making Your Eyes Ache?

When your eyes do not produce enough tears or the tears evaporate too quickly, inflammation builds on the surface of the eye. That inflammation can create a burning, aching sensation that patients often describe as pressure behind the eye.

The National Eye Institute reports that nearly 16.4 million Americans have dry eye. Risk factors include being over 50, being female, wearing contact lenses, and spending extended time on screens. Screen use is especially problematic because research shows your blink rate tends to decrease when you are focused on a monitor, which accelerates tear evaporation.

If your eyes feel worse in air-conditioned offices, on airplanes, or at the end of a long screen session, dry eye may be contributing to your symptoms. The good news is that effective treatments exist at every level, from over-the-counter artificial tears to in-office procedures like intense pulsed light therapy and radio frequency treatment.

Are Your Sinuses Causing Eye Pain?

Your sinuses wrap around your eye socket like a frame around a picture. The frontal sinuses sit above your eyes, the ethmoid sinuses sit between them, and the maxillary sinuses sit below. When any of these cavities become inflamed from allergies, a cold, or a bacterial infection, the swelling creates pressure that radiates directly into the area behind your eyes.

Sinus-related eye pain usually comes with companions: nasal congestion, facial tenderness when you press on your cheeks or forehead, postnasal drip, and sometimes a low-grade fever. The pain often worsens when you bend forward because that position increases pressure in the inflamed sinuses.

If you notice that your eye pain tracks with allergy season, follows a cold, or comes with noticeable congestion, sinuses are a likely culprit. Nasal saline rinses and steam inhalation can bring relief, and your primary care doctor can advise on appropriate medications if needed. Persistent sinus symptoms lasting more than 10 days may need evaluation by your primary care doctor to determine whether antibiotics or further workup is needed.

Could a Migraine Be Behind the Pain?

Migraine is one of the most common neurological conditions in the world, affecting approximately 39 million Americans according to the Migraine Research Foundation. Women are disproportionately affected, with 18% of American women experiencing migraine compared to 6% of men.

Retro-orbital pain, the medical term for pain behind the eye, is one of the hallmark symptoms of migraine. The pain is often throbbing, typically affects one side of the head, and can last anywhere from 4 to 72 hours. It frequently comes with nausea, sensitivity to light and sound, and sometimes visual disturbances called aura, which are experienced by about 25% of migraine patients. Aura can include flickering lights, zigzag lines, or temporary blind spots that usually resolve within an hour.

Many patients with migraine initially assume their pain is an eye problem. A comprehensive eye exam can help rule out ocular causes and confirm that the pain pattern is consistent with migraine, so you can pursue the right treatment path with a neurologist or headache specialist.

What Is Optic Neuritis and How Does It Cause Eye Pain?

Optic neuritis is inflammation of the optic nerve, the cable that carries visual signals from your eye to your brain. According to the American Academy of Ophthalmology, the hallmark symptom is pain that worsens when you move your eye, particularly when looking to the side or upward.

Other signs include blurry vision, dimmed or faded colors (as if someone turned down the brightness on a screen), and symptoms that develop suddenly or build over several days. Optic neuritis can worsen with heat or physical exertion, so you might notice your vision gets temporarily worse after exercise or a hot shower.

This condition is associated with autoimmune conditions such as multiple sclerosis (MS) and MOGAD (myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein antibody-associated disease). If you notice pain with eye movement along with vision changes, contact your eye care provider promptly. Treatment with corticosteroids can improve outcomes when started early.

What Are Cluster Headaches and Why Do They Target the Eye?

Cluster headaches are less common than migraine, but they are among the most severe pain conditions known to medicine. They cause intense, burning or piercing pain behind or around one eye, and attacks tend to strike at the same time each day during "cluster periods" that last weeks to months.

During a cluster headache episode, you may notice tearing, redness, or a droopy eyelid on the affected side. The pain is severe enough that patients often describe an inability to sit still, needing to pace or rock. Cluster headaches are relatively uncommon and are more common in men, which is the opposite of the gender pattern seen with migraine.

If you experience severe, recurring pain behind one eye that follows a predictable daily schedule, mention this specific pattern to your doctor. Cluster headaches are treatable with preventive and acute therapies, and identifying the pattern is the first step toward relief.

When Should You Worry About Pain Behind the Eye?

Most pain behind the eye is caused by treatable, non-emergency conditions. However, certain symptoms require prompt professional evaluation.

What Are the Red-Flag Symptoms?

Contact an eye care professional the same day if you experience any of the following along with your eye pain:

  • Sudden changes in vision, including blurring, double vision, or partial vision loss
  • Severe pain accompanied by nausea or vomiting
  • Halos or rainbow-colored rings around lights
  • A visibly red, swollen, or bulging eye
  • Pain following head trauma or an injury to the eye
  • Fever combined with eye pain and redness
  • Pain with eye movement plus vision changes, which could indicate optic neuritis

When Should You Go to the Emergency Room?

The American Academy of Ophthalmology identifies acute angle-closure glaucoma as "a true eye emergency." This condition causes a sudden spike in eye pressure that can lead to permanent vision loss if not treated within hours. Symptoms include severe eye pain, headache, nausea, halos around lights, and sudden blurry vision.

Other situations that warrant emergency care include sudden, complete vision loss in one or both eyes, chemical exposure to the eye, and a penetrating injury.

When in doubt, err on the side of caution. Calling your eye care provider for guidance is always a reasonable step, and most offices can advise you over the phone about whether your symptoms need same-day evaluation.

How Can You Relieve Pain Behind the Eye at Home?

If your symptoms are mild and do not include any of the red flags above, several strategies can help while you decide whether to schedule an appointment.

What Is the 20-20-20 Rule?

The American Optometric Association recommends a simple practice for anyone who spends extended time on screens: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds.

This works because it gives your ciliary muscle a chance to relax. That small muscle inside the eye contracts to focus on near objects like your computer screen, and after hours of sustained contraction, it fatigues. The 20-second break at distance allows it to release and reset. Setting a recurring timer on your phone or computer is one of the simplest ways to build this habit into your workday.

Do Warm Compresses or Artificial Tears Help?

Warm compresses applied to closed eyes for 10 to 15 minutes can help relieve both dry eye discomfort and sinus-related pressure. For dry eye specifically, the warmth helps soften oils in the meibomian glands along your eyelid margins, improving the quality of your tear film.

Preservative-free artificial tears are a safe, over-the-counter option for dry eye relief. They supplement your natural tear film and reduce the surface inflammation that contributes to aching and burning. If you find yourself reaching for drops more than four times a day, it is worth discussing your symptoms with an eye care professional who can evaluate whether a more targeted treatment would help.

Can Hydration and Sleep Make a Difference?

Yes, on both counts. Dehydration reduces tear production, which worsens dry eye symptoms and the associated aching behind the eyes. Drinking adequate water throughout the day, aiming for about eight glasses, supports healthy tear volume.

Sleep deprivation compounds eye strain and lowers your threshold for headaches, including migraine. Your eyes need overnight rest to recover from the demands of daily visual work. Aiming for seven to eight hours of quality sleep and reducing screen brightness in the evening can help your eyes feel noticeably better the next day. If you use devices before bed, consider enabling night mode or blue-light reduction settings to reduce strain.

How Does an Eye Exam Help Identify the Cause?

What Does an Optometrist Check During a Comprehensive Eye Exam?

A comprehensive eye exam is the most efficient way to evaluate pain behind the eye because it covers the most common causes in a single visit. Here is what your optometrist can assess:

  • Refraction: Determines whether your glasses or contact lens prescription is accurate and up to date, ruling out strain from uncorrected vision problems.
  • Tonometry: Measures the pressure inside your eye. This quick, painless test can identify elevated pressure associated with glaucoma.
  • Dilated eye exam: Allows your optometrist to examine the optic nerve, retina, and blood vessels at the back of the eye, checking for signs of optic neuritis, diabetic retinopathy, or other abnormalities.
  • Tear film assessment: Evaluates tear quantity and quality to diagnose dry eye and determine the best treatment approach.
  • Ocular motility: Tests how your eyes move and work together, identifying coordination issues that can contribute to strain and discomfort.

If your optometrist determines that the cause falls outside the scope of eye care, such as migraine management or sinus treatment, they can refer you to the appropriate specialist with specific findings to guide your next steps. Starting with an eye exam means you arrive at that specialist's office with answers, not just questions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why Does Pain Behind My Eye Get Worse When I Move It?

Pain that intensifies with eye movement is a clinical hallmark of optic neuritis, an inflammatory condition of the optic nerve. It can also occur with orbital inflammation or severe sinusitis. If you notice this pattern, especially if it comes with changes in your vision or color perception, schedule an eye exam promptly so your provider can evaluate the optic nerve directly.

Can Pain Behind the Eye Be a Sign of Something Serious?

In most cases, pain behind the eye is caused by benign, treatable conditions like eye strain, dry eye, or sinus congestion. Rarely, it can signal something more urgent, such as acute glaucoma, optic neuritis, or a vascular event. The red-flag symptoms listed above are your guide. If you have any of those warning signs, seek same-day care.

Should I See an Eye Doctor or My Primary Care Doctor First?

For pain behind the eye, an optometrist is often the most efficient first call. A comprehensive eye exam can evaluate or rule out the most common causes, including refractive error, dry eye, elevated eye pressure, and optic nerve health, in a single appointment. If the cause turns out to be sinus-related, neurological, or systemic, your optometrist will refer you to the right specialist with specific clinical findings that help speed up your diagnosis.

Is Pain Behind the Eye Related to High Blood Pressure?

Severe, uncontrolled high blood pressure can affect the blood vessels in the retina, a condition called hypertensive retinopathy. In rare cases, this can contribute to headaches and visual changes. However, mild-to-moderate high blood pressure does not typically cause noticeable eye pain. If you have hypertension and are experiencing persistent eye pain or vision changes, a dilated eye exam can check your retinal blood vessels for signs of damage.

Ready to Find Out What Is Causing Your Eye Pain?

Pain behind the eye can be frustrating and worrying, but identifying the cause is the first step toward lasting relief. Whether your symptoms point to digital eye strain, dry eye, or something that needs closer evaluation, a comprehensive eye exam can give you answers.
