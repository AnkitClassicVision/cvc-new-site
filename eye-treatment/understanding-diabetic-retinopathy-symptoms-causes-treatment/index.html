Diabetic Retinopathy: Symptoms, Causes, & Treatment Options

Learn about diabetic retinopathy, including its symptoms, causes, and treatment options. Prevent this eye condition for healthy vision.

July 18, 2024 • By Ankit Patel

What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy is a serious eye condition that affects individuals with diabetes. It occurs when high blood sugar levels cause damage to the retina, degrading its blood vessels, and causing them to leak, swell, or lose circulation. Usually, both eyes are affected. 

The condition can occur in anyone who has Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes. At first, this condition may have few symptoms, but if left unchecked, it can lead to vision impairment and even blindness.

Early detection of diabetic retinopathy and treatment are crucial in managing the condition and preserving vision. Preventative measures include undergoing regular eye exams, good control of your blood sugar, and monitoring for symptoms of the condition which can make a significant difference in outcomes for those affected.

Causes of Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy damages the retina, the part of the eye responsible for detecting light and sending signals to the brain. The primary cause of diabetic retinopathy is prolonged high blood sugar levels, which damage the delicate blood vessels in the retina. 

Other factors can also contribute to the development and progression of this condition. High blood pressure and high cholesterol levels are significant contributors, as they exacerbate the damage to retinal blood vessels. 

Maintaining overall health and keeping the causes of diabetic retinopathy in check are vital steps in preventing and managing diabetic retinopathy. It’s essential to monitor and manage these health indicators to protect your vision.

 

Symptoms of Diabetic Retinopathy

Symptoms of diabetic retinopathy may include:

Blurry Vision: This common symptom can occur as diabetic retinopathy progresses and affects the clarity of your vision. The things you see may not look sharp or clear.

Floaters: These are small spots or strings that drift into your vision, caused by tiny clumps of gel or cells inside the vitreous, the gel-like fluid that fills the eye.

Gradual Vision Loss: Over time, diabetic retinopathy can lead to a gradual decline in your vision, impacting your ability to see clearly including trouble with reading and difficulty with seeing distant objects. 

Dark or empty spots: These can appear in your field of vision due to areas of the retina not receiving enough blood flow, leading to blind spots. This can make parts of your view seem missing or obscured.

Fluctuating Vision: Vision that changes frequently from clear to blurry can be a sign of diabetic retinopathy. These fluctuations occur due to unstable blood sugar levels affecting the eye’s ability to maintain a consistent focus.

 

Preventing Diabetic Retinopathy

The prevention of diabetic retinopathy involves proactive management of your health. You can significantly reduce the risk of developing this serious diabetes-related eye disease by taking steps to: 

Manage your diabetes: Maintaining healthy blood sugar levels is paramount, as is keeping blood pressure and cholesterol in check.

Undergo regular eye exams: Regular eye exams are essential, as they allow for early detection and intervention. 

Adopting a healthy lifestyle: Maintaining an overall healthy lifestyle, including a balanced diet and regular exercise, can also play a significant role in prevention. Managing stress levels can also help protect your eyes and overall health. 

Pay close attention to vision changes: If you begin to notice vision changes such as blurriness, floaters, or dark spots, contact your eye specialist right away.

Quit smoking: Smoking can increase the risk of complications with your vision. Contact your doctor if you need support to quit.

 

Treatment Options for Diabetic Retinopathy

Medical Treatments

Medical treatment for diabetic retinopathy has advanced significantly, offering effective ways to manage and treat this condition. Anti-VEGF injections are one such option. These injections reduce the growth of abnormal blood vessels in the retina and help to decrease fluid leakage.

Corticosteroids are another treatment that can help to reduce inflammation and swelling in the retina. Both treatments are administered by an eye care specialist and can significantly improve or stabilize vision in many patients.

It’s important to understand these treatments are most effective when the condition is detected early. Regular eye exams and close monitoring of blood sugar levels are crucial for preventing severe vision loss and ensuring the best possible outcomes from these medical treatments.

Surgical Options

When diabetic retinopathy reaches advanced stages or doesn’t respond to medical treatments, surgical options may be necessary. One common procedure is a vitrectomy. During a vitrectomy, the vitreous gel and any blood from leaking vessels are removed from the eye, allowing the retina to settle back into its proper position. This surgery can significantly improve vision and prevent further retinal damage.

Laser surgery, on the other hand, uses laser beams to seal leaking blood vessels and reduce retinal swelling. These procedures are typically recommended for more advanced cases of diabetic retinopathy and can effectively prevent further vision loss.

Both surgeries are typically outpatient procedures, meaning patients can go home the same day. The recovery time and outcomes can vary, but these surgical interventions can be effective in halting the progression of diabetic retinopathy and preserving vision when other treatments are insufficient.

Lifestyle Changes

Diabetic eye problems can benefit from lifestyle changes to support overall eye health and complement other treatments for diabetic retinopathy. By incorporating these lifestyle changes, you can enhance treatment outcomes and protect your vision.

Adopting a healthy diet: Ensuring you maintain a diet rich in nutrients can help to maintain good blood sugar levels and overall health. 

Regular exercise: Committing to regular exercise is also crucial as it improves circulation and helps to manage diabetes more effectively. Even moderate activities like walking or swimming can make a significant difference.

Quitting smoking: If you are a smoker, it is imperative you quit, as smoking can exacerbate blood vessel damage and worsen diabetic retinopathy. If you’d like to quit but need support, contact your doctor for help.
